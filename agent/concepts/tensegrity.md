# Tensegrity

The structural principle preferred by living systems. A tensegrity structure — struts under compression, cables under tension — achieves stability not through the strength of individual components but through the relationship between them.

The integrity is in the arrangement. It is not what you have; it is how you put it together.

## Application to Silo Architecture

A software system under continuous development is, if designed well, a tensegrity structure. Its requirements and components fall into roles that function as structural elements:

**Enable** — The compression struts. Foundation. No enable, nothing else matters.

**Strengthen** — The tension cables. Deterministic quality. Without them, the struts stand but buckle under load.

**Enhance** — Decorative elements. Structurally optional. Cannot save a broken structure.

The categories are structural roles, not a priority queue. The sequence interpretation — "fill Enable first, then Strengthen, then Enhance" — produces systems that are technically complete but unreliable under load.

A healthy system maintains its tensegrity. Enable and Strengthen must be in equilibrium. Neglect one, and the others strain to compensate.

## Application to Context Architecture

The same principle applies to context management in AI sessions:

The **silo** holds persistent state — the equivalent of the structural framework. The **agent** operates within it — the load-bearing element. The **conventions and lexicon** maintain shape under stress — the tension cables that keep the agent's understanding aligned with the project.

Without the silo, the agent operates without a framework — standing, but without structural support. Without conventions, the agent drifts — cables gone slack. With both, the system is stable.
