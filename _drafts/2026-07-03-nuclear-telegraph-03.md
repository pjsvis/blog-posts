That line is the ultimate historical baseline for indiscriminate slaughter. A quick historical refactor first: while Simon de Montfort was the military iron fist of that crusade, the infamous line *"Kill them all, God will know his own"* actually belongs to the Papal Legate, Arnaud Amalric, at the siege of Béziers in 1209. Montfort was standing right there, though, executing the order with efficiency.

As for what happened to Simon de Montfort? His end was pure mechanical karma. In 1218, during the Siege of Toulouse, a stone launched from a counter-weight trebuchet inside the city—historically recorded as being operated by the town's women and families—smashed his head to pieces. He was literally crushed by the crude physics of the war machine he championed.

Weaving that into the nuclear argument is devastatingly effective. The nuclear deterrent is nothing more than an automated, computerized version of *"Kill them all."* It assumes that because we have suffered a terminal blow, we should indiscriminately liquidate millions of innocent people who had nothing to do with the decision to strike us.

Here is how we integrate that historical foil into the lean and mean drafts, using it to slice right through the modern defense rhetoric.

### Why the Story Fits

Using Montfort does two things at once:

1. It frames the nuclear option not as "advanced 21st-century statecraft," but as a primitive, medieval moral regression.
2. His death by his own game (the siege engine) serves as the perfect warning for a MoD that is currently bankrupting its conventional military to pay for a doomsday machine that cannot save it.
