# UK Defence Investment Plan (June 2026) — Analysis

## 1. Overall Spending Profile

Defence spending is rising from approximately 60bn (2024/25) to approximately 73.5bn annually by 2028/29. The GDP trajectory is: 2.5% by 2027, 3% next parliament, a NATO commitment of 3.5% by 2035.

However, the MoD's 2023-2033 Equipment Plan carries a 19bn shortfall — the largest since records began in 2012. Defence Secretary John Healey resigned on 11 June 2026, stating the PM was "unable, and the Treasury unwilling, to commit the resources that the nation needs." Dan Jarvis took over and refocused the DIP toward "immediate frontline priorities."

## 2. Big-Ticket / Legacy Platform Spending

- **Dreadnought (nuclear submarines):** approximately 31bn+. Consumes about 38% of the Equipment Plan, about 20% of total defence spend. Single largest item.
- **Tempest / GCAP (next-gen fighter):** multi-billion. UK/Italy/Japan joint programme.
- **Ajax armoured vehicles:** extra 250mn being diverted. Deeply troubled history; PAC warns of "spending even more than planned in hope of salvaging something."
- **Type 26 frigates:** multi-billion. Ongoing build programme.
- **Nuclear Enterprise (overall):** dominates. Single largest cost to MoD — structurally crowds out conventional modernisation.

## 3. Modern Warfare Allocations (Ukraine/Iran-informed)

- **Drones and autonomous systems (all 3 services):** 5bn headline figure. Biggest single modern warfare line item.
- **Commando Force transformation:** 500mn. High-speed Joint Commando Craft (with Norway), uncrewed vessels, next-gen comms, networked targeting, strike drones. About 100mn for transformative tech.
- **British Army drones and uncrewed vehicles:** 50mn. Specific army allocation.
- **RAF autonomous fighters:** part of broader 5bn. "Loyal wingman" type development.
- **New larger Amphibious Ships:** joint fleet with Netherlands.

Summary: approximately 5.5bn+ explicitly allocated to drones, autonomous systems, commando modernisation, and uncrewed platforms — versus legacy programmes totalling tens of billions (Dreadnought alone is 31bn+).

## 4. Key Criticisms and Tensions

1. Chatham House (June 2026): "Dishonesty is endemic" in UK defence strategy — optimism bias and self-deception about what is affordable.
2. Al Carns (former Veterans Minister): publicly criticised MoD waste and sunk costs in legacy programmes; argued armoured vehicle spending should be reconsidered for systems better suited to modern warfare.
3. Structural crowding out: the nuclear deterrent's disproportionate share (about 20%) structurally crowds out conventional modernisation — a tension no DIP can fully resolve without revisiting Trident.
4. Treasury-MoD doom loop: Treasury resists larger budgets when MoD is seen as inefficient; underfunding breeds inefficiency which justifies further underfunding.
5. PAC warning: DIP delays were "hindering modernisation" and "undermining relationships with defence industry" — particularly damaging for innovative SMEs in the drone/AI space.
6. Credibility gap: the SDR committed to "warfighting readiness" but the funding gap undermines credibility.

## 5. Opinion: Is the Balance Right?

No — the modern warfare allocations are in the right direction but an order of magnitude too small, and the DIP cannot resolve the structural problem on its own.

The nuclear deterrent consumes about 20% of all defence spending. That is a political choice from the 2015 Trident renewal decision, and no DIP can unwind it. Everything else — including the 5bn drone pot — competes for the remaining 80% against legacy contracts (Ajax, Tempest, Type 26) that are already in train and politically painful to cancel.

The 5bn for drones and autonomous systems sounds significant, but in context:

- It represents approximately 7% of the projected 73.5bn annual budget — spread across all three services and multiple years.
- Dreadnought alone costs more than 6x that entire pot.
- The PAC's 19bn equipment plan shortfall means the MoD does not actually know how it pays for what it is already committed to, let alone new priorities.

The Treasury-MoD doom loop is the core problem: the Treasury will not give more money while the MoD is seen as inefficient, and the MoD cannot reform while it is underfunded. The DIP is a repackaging exercise — shifting some money toward drones and commando modernisation (genuinely positive moves) — without confronting the Dreadnought question or the Ajax sunk-cost trap.

Recommendation: the 5bn drone fund should be 15-20bn, and the MoD needs a proper defence review that asks whether continuous-at-sea nuclear deterrence at its current scale is compatible with conventional modernisation — because right now, it is not.
