# The S-1 and the Perky Pat Layout: What SpaceX's IPO Filing Doesn't Say About the Human Body in Space

On May 20, 2026, SpaceX filed its S-1 registration with the SEC. The document runs 377 pages. It contains $18.67 billion in 2025 revenue, a $4.9 billion net loss, $20.7 billion in capital expenditures — 60% of which went to AI — and 36 pages of risk factors. It proposes a valuation of $1.75 trillion, which would make it the largest IPO in Wall Street history by a factor of five. It includes a CEO compensation package that vests in tranches tied not to revenue or profit but to the establishment of "a permanent human colony on Mars with at least one million inhabitants."

The financial press has done its job. The revenue breakdown is covered. The Starlink subscriber count — 10.3 million across 164 countries — is widely reported. The dual-class share structure granting Elon Musk 85.1% voting control has been noted. The $28.5 trillion total addressable market claim has been quoted, often with a raised eyebrow.

What nobody is talking about is what the S-1 *doesn't* say. Not because the information is hidden — SpaceX's risk factors are voluminous and candid — but because the most important deficit in the entire filing is not financial. It is biological. And it is structural. And it makes the Mars colony milestone not merely ambitious but, in the current architectural paradigm, a guarantee of mass physiological collapse.

---

## The Perky Pat Layout

In Philip K. Dick's 1965 novel *The Three Stigmata of Palmer Eldritch*, human colonists on Mars live in underground hovels. The environment is lethal. The accommodations are grim. The colonists survive by taking a drug called Can-D, which allows them to temporarily inhabit the bodies of miniature dolls — Perky Pat and her boyfriend Walt — living in a perfect, sunlit, Earth-like diorama called the Perky Pat layout. The layout is detailed, immersive, and entirely fake. It is a constructed reality that makes the actual reality bearable.

The SpaceX S-1 is a Perky Pat layout.

The diorama is magnificent. Revenue curves slope upward. Starship promises 99% cost reduction to orbit. Starlink throws off $7.2 billion in segment EBITDA at 63% margins. The TAM stretches to $28.5 trillion. The language is elevated — "extend the light of consciousness to the stars" is in the mission statement. The financial architecture is intricate, the underwriting syndicate runs 22 banks deep, and the roadshow begins June 4 with an expected listing June 12 under ticker SPCX.

This is the layout. It is detailed. It is immersive. And it is designed to distract you from the hovel.

The hovel, in this case, is what happens to the human body in space. Not in a few years. Not when we figure it out. Right now. Today. In the vehicles SpaceX currently operates and the ones it has on the drawing board.

## Ninety Days

The International Space Station has been continuously occupied since November 2000. In that quarter-century, NASA and its international partners have compiled an exhaustive record of what microgravity does to the human body. The findings are not ambiguous.

At approximately 90 days of continuous microgravity exposure, the human body begins a process of systemic degradation that current countermeasures cannot arrest:

**Bone density** decreases at roughly 1-2% per month in weight-bearing skeletal regions. This is not linear — it accelerates. A six-month ISS rotation can produce bone loss equivalent to a decade of osteoporosis on Earth. The calcium leached from bone enters the bloodstream, increasing kidney stone risk. Some bone architecture never fully recovers after return.

**Muscle atrophy** is rapid and severe. Postural muscles — the ones that keep you upright against gravity — lose mass and function within weeks. Astronauts on six-month missions lose up to 20% of muscle mass despite two hours of daily resistance exercise. The exercise equipment required to achieve even this partial mitigation — the Advanced Resistive Exercise Device on the ISS — is a vibration-isolated, piston-driven machine the size of a refrigerator.

**Cardiovascular deconditioning** occurs within days. The heart, relieved of the need to pump blood against gravity, remodels itself. Cardiac muscle atrophies. Blood volume decreases. Upon return to gravity, astronauts experience orthostatic intolerance — they cannot stand without fainting. This resolves over weeks on Earth. It would be fatal on a planetary surface after a multi-month transit.

**Vision impairment** — Spaceflight-Associated Neuro-ocular Syndrome, or SANS — affects approximately 70% of astronauts on long-duration missions. The mechanism involves fluid shift toward the head in microgravity, which flattens the back of the eyeball, swells the optic nerve, and produces folds in the retina. Some astronauts experience permanent visual changes. The cause is understood. The solution is not.

**Immune system dysregulation** is documented but poorly understood. Latent viruses reactivate. Wound healing slows. T-cell function is suppressed. The combined effect is an astronaut whose body is simultaneously breaking down its structural elements and losing its capacity to repair them.

This is not speculation. This is the physiological record of the 270 people who have visited the ISS. And this is in low Earth orbit, protected by Earth's magnetosphere from the worst of galactic cosmic radiation, with near-immediate evacuation capability and constant ground-based medical monitoring.

A Mars transit, by contrast, is six to nine months each way. It occurs outside the magnetosphere. There is no evacuation capability. There is no ARED. And upon arrival — after the body has spent the better part of a year in freefall — the crew must function in 38% of Earth's gravity on a planetary surface with no medical infrastructure whatsoever.

## The Hovel, Up Close

The biological degradation is bad enough. But biology is only half of what makes the freefall transit a hovel. The other half is psychological, and it is rarely discussed in polite aerospace company.

Consider the actual living conditions of a Starship crew during a 6-9 month Mars transit. The habitable volume of the crew compartment — even in the most generous configurations SpaceX has described — is roughly comparable to a studio apartment. For multiple people. For the better part of a year. There is no private space that cannot be invaded by sound. There is no fresh air. There is no window you can open. The atmosphere is recirculated through filters that accumulate the collective biological output of the crew — breath, sweat, flatulence, the slow seep of human existence — and return it slightly cleaner than it was before. It never smells good. After the first month, you stop noticing. After the third, you would kill for the smell of rain.

The toilet is a vacuum system. It works most of the time. When it doesn't, the crew performs maintenance in a confined volume where everyone can hear and smell the result. This is not speculation. ISS astronauts have described fecal containment failures in terms that would make a plumber wince. On the ISS, a repair crew arrives with the next resupply mission in weeks. On a Mars transit, there is no resupply mission. There is no repair crew. There is you, a wrench, and the certain knowledge that if you fail, everyone lives with the consequence.

Boredom is not trivial. The first weeks are novel — you're in space, the views are spectacular, the mission is historic. By month three, the novelty has curdled into routine. The same compartments. The same faces. The same food, reconstituted from pouches, eaten without the sensory pleasure of smell (microgravity congests the sinuses, dulling taste). The same exercise regimen, two hours a day on a machine that fights you because there's no gravity to fight. The same conversations, circling the same topics, because there is nothing new to talk about — nothing has happened except the slow accumulation of distance between you and everything you have ever loved.

Psychological research on isolated, confined, controlled environments — submarine crews, Antarctic overwinter teams, long-duration spaceflight — identifies a predictable progression. After the initial excitement phase, crews enter a period of interpersonal tension, irritability, and declining motivation known as the "third-quarter phenomenon." Minor irritations become major conflicts. A crewmate who chews too loudly becomes an object of obsessive hatred. A joke that was funny the first time becomes unbearable by the fiftieth repetition. Crew cohesion degrades. Performance degrades. In extreme cases, crews have reported periods where members stopped speaking to each other entirely — in a volume the size of a studio apartment, for weeks at a time.

And this is with carefully selected, psychologically screened, professionally trained astronauts operating in low Earth orbit with constant ground support. The Mars transit crew will be subject to the same confinement stressors with the added burden of radiation exposure, physiological decay, and the certain knowledge that there is no way home for at least two years.

The S-1 does not discuss any of this. Not because it is speculative — the psychological literature on isolated confined environments is decades deep and uncontroversial. Because, like the biological degradation, it is not a financial risk until it produces a financial consequence. The first Mars crew that arrives with members in a state of mutual psychological collapse will produce that consequence. The S-1 will be revised. A footnote will be added. The footnote will be too late.

## What the S-1 Says — and Doesn't

The S-1 is not silent on biological risk. Item 1A, Risk Factors, runs 36 pages and includes candid language about "catastrophic accidents," "human injury or death," and the physical hazards of spaceflight. But the biological degradation I've described above is not treated as a structural engineering problem requiring a structural engineering solution. It is treated as an operational hazard to be managed.

The distinction is the argument. An operational hazard can be mitigated: exercise protocols, nutritional supplementation, crew selection. A structural engineering problem must be *solved*: you build a spacecraft that produces gravity, or you accept that your colonists will arrive broken. SpaceX's S-1 treats microgravity physiology as the former. The physiological record says it is the latter. If microgravity-induced physiological decay is an operational hazard, you manage it with exercise protocols, nutritional supplementation, and crew selection — you select the fittest candidates and hope their bodies hold up. If it is a *structural engineering problem*, you build a spacecraft that produces gravity.

SpaceX's current architecture does not produce gravity. Dragon, the crew capsule that has carried 78 astronauts from 20 countries since 2020, is a freefall vehicle. Starship, the next-generation rocket on which the entire growth case rests, is designed as a freefall vehicle for transit phases. The mission architecture described in the S-1 — launch, transit, landing — treats the human body as cargo that will be delivered intact.

The physiological record says otherwise. A crew arriving on Mars after eight months in freefall will not be intact. They will be osteoporotic, sarcopenic, immunocompromised, and unable to stand unassisted in 0.38g. They will have arrived at the most hostile environment ever encountered by humans in the worst physical condition of their lives.

This is not a failure of ambition. It is a failure of architecture.

## The Numbers That Actually Matter

The S-1 contains many numbers. $18.67 billion in revenue. $1.75 trillion in proposed valuation. 15 compensation tranches. 1 million colonists. Here are the numbers it doesn't contain:

**0.0g** — the gravitational environment of the transit vehicle.

**90 days** — the approximate point at which systemic physiological degradation becomes medically significant.

**6-9 months** — the one-way Mars transit duration using current propulsion technology.

**70%** — the proportion of long-duration astronauts who develop vision impairment from fluid shift.

**0** — the number of rotating-habitat spacecraft currently in development at SpaceX.

**1** — the number of humans who have ever spent more than 14 consecutive months in microgravity (Valeri Polyakov, Mir, 1994-1995).

**0** — the number of humans who have ever experienced the combined insult of interplanetary radiation exposure and microgravity-induced physiological decay simultaneously.

These are not risk factors. These are constraints. You cannot manage a constraint with exercise protocols. You solve it with architecture, or you accept that your colonists will arrive broken.

## Why Nobody Is Asking

The financial press covers what the filing says. The filing says revenue, TAM, valuation, voting rights, risk factors. It does not say: "We have not solved the problem of keeping humans alive and functional during the transit phase of our primary mission."

This is not a conspiracy. It is a category error. The S-1 is a financial document, and financial documents describe financial realities. The biological reality of spaceflight is not a financial reality until it becomes a liability — which it will, the first time a crew arrives on Mars unable to perform basic motor functions, or the first time a Starship returns to Earth carrying astronauts with permanent vision loss.

The engineering press covers what the rockets can do. Starship's payload capacity, its reusability, its engine configuration, its test flight record. What it doesn't cover is that Starship — like every spacecraft ever built — is a freefall vehicle. It gets you there. It does not keep you alive while doing so.

This leaves a void. The financial analysts don't have the physiology. The aerospace engineers don't have the biology. The medical researchers don't have the access. And so the most important question about the largest IPO in history — *can the humans actually survive the product?* — goes unasked.

## The Claustrophobia Before the Cathedral

I want to be precise about what I am and am not arguing here.

I am not arguing that SpaceX is fraudulent. The S-1 is transparent about its risks within the category of risks it considers. The company has achieved things — reusable orbital-class rockets, a global satellite broadband network, the first commercial crew delivery to the ISS — that were widely considered impossible.

I am not arguing that Mars is unreachable. I am arguing that the *current architectural paradigm* — freefall transit, no artificial gravity, no structural mitigation of microgravity effects — is incompatible with the stated mission of establishing a permanent human presence there. You cannot colonize a planet with colonists who cannot walk when they arrive.

I am not arguing that the problem is unsolvable. I am arguing that it is unsolved — and more than unsolved, it is *unaddressed* in the single most important document SpaceX has ever produced about its future.

The S-1 is the Perky Pat layout. The revenue curves, the TAM claims, the compensation tranches, the underwriting syndicate — all of it is documented, and all of it is detailed, and all of it is designed to convince you that the diorama is the territory. But the territory is a hovel. The hovel is the human body in freefall. And nobody is talking about it.

And *why* nobody is talking about it — why a company founded by the world's most ambitious industrialist has not addressed the single most tractable constraint on its primary mission — is the subject of [Part 2 of this series](/blog-posts/posts/2026-05-25-spacex-borg-crucible-janeway/).
