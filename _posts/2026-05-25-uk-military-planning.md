# The Wrong War: Lessons from Ukraine and the UK's Defence Procurement Choices

The conflict in Ukraine has provided one of the clearest empirical stress tests of modern warfare in decades. The results are uncomfortable for legacy military planning: low-cost, rapidly iterated autonomous systems have repeatedly demonstrated high impact against expensive, complex platforms. Yet the UK's current defence procurement direction risks repeating a familiar pattern — prioritising prestige platforms over scalable, economically productive capability.

This is not a failure of ambition. It is a classic misalignment between incentives, metrics, and the territory.

---

## The Empirical Record

In Ukraine, we have seen a smaller force achieve disproportionate effects through rapid production and adaptation of cheap drones — reportedly scaling to millions of units annually. Expensive interceptors and traditional armour have struggled with cost-exchange ratios that heavily favour the attacker. The math is brutal: when your defensive munition costs orders of magnitude more than the incoming threat, stockpiles deplete quickly and industrial base strain becomes acute.

These patterns are not anomalies. They reflect a broader shift toward attritable, decentralised systems in contested environments. The UK's 2025 Strategic Defence Review and its 2026 implementation updates acknowledge the need for "lethality through autonomy," but the capital allocation tells a different story.

Major commitments remain anchored in long-cycle, high-unit-cost programmes:
- Expansion of the SSN-AUKUS submarine fleet
- Continued investment in Tempest sixth-generation fighter
- Legacy armoured vehicle modernisation (Challenger 3, Ajax, Boxer)

These are impressive technical achievements. They are also slow to deliver, expensive to maintain, and represent concentrated bets on a small number of Tier-1 primes. The opportunity cost is real.

---

## The Economic Dimension

National defence spending is rising toward 2.5% of GDP by 2027 and potentially 3% thereafter. This represents a significant increase in public capital — roughly £6 billion additional per year. Where that capital flows will shape the UK's productive capacity for the next generation.

Two broad paths exist:

**Path A (Current Trajectory)**: Capital flows primarily into long-horizon megaprojects and imported components. Fiscal multipliers are low. Much of the spending leaks overseas or locks into slow R&D cycles with limited civilian spillover. SME participation remains low.

**Path B (Alternative)**: Treat defence procurement as a deliberate industrial strategy. Build a distributed, high-velocity ecosystem that integrates Ukraine's battle-tested innovation model with the UK's regional strengths:
- Scotland’s photonics, data science, and space clusters for AI navigation and sensing
- Wales’ semiconductor and advanced electronics base for component manufacturing
- Northern Ireland’s cyber and precision engineering clusters for software-hardware integration

This approach would treat defence as a rapid-cycle innovation engine rather than a series of sovereign monuments. Capital recirculates domestically through SMEs. Iteration happens in weeks, not decades. Technological spillovers into the civilian economy become plausible.

---

## Incentives and Structural Realities

The preference for exquisite platforms is understandable. They are visible, politically salient, and align with traditional notions of military prestige. They also suit established procurement bureaucracies and prime contractors. However, they optimise for peacetime signalling rather than wartime attrition economics.

Ukraine has shown that in prolonged high-intensity conflict, velocity and cost discipline often matter more than individual platform sophistication. A union-wide "wartime lab" model — deliberately decentralised, regionally distributed, and focused on component-level sovereignty — would better align defence needs with the UK's broader economic objective of sustainable growth.

This is not about abandoning conventional capability. It is about recognising that the binding constraint in 2026 is not merely headline spending, but the productivity and resilience of the industrial base that spending supports.
