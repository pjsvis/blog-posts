# What SpaceX's IPO Filing Doesn't Say About the Human Body in Space

On May 20, 2026, SpaceX filed its S-1 registration statement. The document is lengthy, the numbers impressive: roughly $18.67 billion in 2025 revenue (heavily Starlink-driven), a proposed valuation approaching $1.75 trillion, and a CEO compensation structure with tranches tied to the establishment of a permanent human colony on Mars numbering at least one million inhabitants. Risk factors are candid where they address hardware and operations. The financial press has covered the valuation, the dual-class shares, and the TAM claims.

What remains underexamined is the territory the filing does not map: the biological constraints of long-duration spaceflight under the current architectural paradigm.

This is not hidden malice. It is a category error. An S-1 is a financial and legal document. It describes risks within the categories investors and regulators expect. Biology sits awkwardly between engineering and medicine—acknowledged in risk factors but not treated as the structural constraint it represents for colony-scale ambitions.

---

## The Physiological Record

A quarter-century of continuous ISS operations has produced an unambiguous dataset. In microgravity:

- Bone density in weight-bearing regions declines at roughly 1–2% per month, accelerating over time. Calcium mobilisation raises kidney stone risk; full recovery is not guaranteed.
- Muscle atrophy, particularly in postural groups, reaches 10–20% over six months despite daily resistance exercise.
- Cardiovascular deconditioning occurs rapidly; orthostatic intolerance upon gravity re-exposure is common.
- Spaceflight-Associated Neuro-ocular Syndrome (SANS) affects a majority of long-duration crew, with fluid shifts producing lasting visual changes in some cases.
- Immune dysregulation and delayed wound healing compound the structural losses.

These effects become medically significant around the 90-day mark under current countermeasures. A Mars transit exceeds this window in freefall, outside Earth’s magnetosphere, with no rapid return option. Upon arrival in 0.38g, the crew must function with minimal medical infrastructure.

This does not render Mars unreachable. It does mean the current freefall transit architecture treats human physiology as an operational hazard to be managed rather than a structural parameter to be solved. For early missions with small, highly selected crews, aggressive countermeasures (exercise, nutrition, selection, pharmacology) may suffice at acceptable risk. For a self-sustaining colony of millions, the scaling properties change. Transit becomes a medical conditioning process whose outputs must still perform as colonists.

## The Architectural Gap

SpaceX’s vehicles—Dragon and Starship in its baseline configuration—are freefall systems optimised for transport. That is rational for current priorities. Yet Starship’s payload revolution (projected ~100 tonnes to orbit at low marginal cost) alters the economics of what can be flown. Minimalist "shipping container" habitats are no longer the only rational response.

With deployable tensegrity structures and large inertial mass from propulsion modules, new design space opens: habitats that provide targeted gravitational loading without rotating the entire volume. Per-person or distributed gravity interventions—whether small rotating tori for sleep or perimeter gravity sleds—become mass-feasible. The body appears to require meaningful mechanical loading primarily during rest and repair windows; zero-g during active hours is tolerable and, in some respects, liberating once terrestrial assumptions (floors, chairs, convection) are stripped away.

The S-1 does not discuss gravitational architecture as a mission requirement. That absence is the point of this series. It is not evidence of failure but of prioritisation under uncertainty. The Edinburgh Protocol lens asks: does the current map accurately reflect the territory? Where are the high-leverage improvements?
