# Why the Biological Deficit Persists: Incentives and Mission Architecture

[Part 1](/blog-posts/2026/05/26/spacex-s1-perky-pat-layout/) established that prolonged microgravity during a Mars transit represents a structural constraint on human performance, not merely an operational risk. The S-1 acknowledges hazards but treats physiology as something to be managed rather than engineered out of the critical path. This post examines *why* this gap has endured despite the company's engineering capability and capital resources.

The answer lies in incentive structures, not individual intent.

---

## The Compensation Metric

Page 235 of the S-1 details the CEO performance award: up to 1 billion Class B shares in 15 tranches. Vesting requires *both* market capitalisation thresholds *and* the establishment and maintenance of "a permanent human colony on Mars with at least one million inhabitants."

This is a binary milestone. It asks: Does the colony exist at the required scale? It does not ask: Do the colonists arrive functionally intact? Can they work effectively upon arrival? What is the medical burden in the early years? How does transit physiology scale to thousands, then millions?

Binary metrics are powerful because they are clear. They reduce ambiguity and focus effort. They are also dangerous precisely because they are clear. They optimise for what is measured and deprioritise what is not (a pattern long familiar to students of management and economics).

In systems terms, this is a classic Goodhart's Law problem: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. The milestone rewards existence over quality of outcome. A colony of one million people meets the criterion even if a non-trivial fraction arrive with significant deconditioning requiring extended recovery in 0.38g.

---

## Governance and Feedback Loops

SpaceX operates with an 85.1% voting control structure via Class B shares. The board is not required to maintain the independent committees typical of public companies. This alignment between founder and voting power accelerates decision-making — a documented strength in execution — but it also weakens external pressure to refine milestones once set.

Investors buying into the IPO are purchasing the Mars narrative. Underwriters are incentivised to close the deal. Regulators review disclosure of risks, not the completeness of engineering roadmaps. None of these parties has strong structural reason to insist on gravitational architecture as a precondition for the colony milestone.

The result is a self-reinforcing loop: hardware progress (Starship reusability, payload capacity) is highly visible and rewarded in both market and internal metrics. Human-factors architecture — tensegrity habitats, targeted artificial gravity, zero-g native design — is slower, less photogenic, and not required to hit the binary target. It therefore receives less attention.

This is not unique to SpaceX. Large technical organisations routinely optimise for what their incentive systems can easily measure. The Scottish Enlightenment thinkers observed similar dynamics in mercantilist trade: incentives shaped what merchants and governments prioritised, sometimes at the expense of long-term resilience (e.g., human cargo outcomes on long voyages).

---

## The Architectural Implication

The current baseline — freefall transit with exercise countermeasures — is a rational response to present constraints and priorities. Starship's payload economics, however, changes the feasible solution space. As discussed in Part 1, deployable tensegrity structures mated to propulsion mass open practical avenues for distributed gravitational loading: perimeter gravity sleds, per-person rotating tori for sleep, or hybrid systems. These need not rotate the entire habitat and need not impose prohibitive mass penalties.

The incentive structure does not *prevent* such work. It simply does not require it for milestone success. That distinction matters. Organisations respond to what is rewarded. If functional arrival of colonists is not part of the success criterion, it remains secondary.

This is a solvable systems problem. Better metrics could include:
- Percentage of crew arriving with performance within specified physiological envelopes.
- Transit medical burden as a design parameter.
- Demonstrated gravitational interventions at subscale (orbital testbeds).

## Toward Improvement

Part 1 framed the problem through the lens of human physiology as critical "stuff" that must become functional "things." This post shows how incentives shape whether that transformation is treated as a core engineering challenge.

Subsequent posts move from diagnosis to prescription. We will examine concrete architectural responses — lightweight tensegrity habitats, inertial mass from drive cylinders, and distributed gravity interventions — that address the constraint directly. The goal is not criticism for its own sake, but clearer maps and better steam engines.

The reframe is straightforward: what assumptions are we carrying? What does first-principles design actually require once mass is treated as the primary currency? The answers sit in the architecture that follows.

Starship economics makes targeted gravity solutions tractable. As **[Post 0](/blog-posts/2026/05/26-zero-g-explainer/)** and the **Bio-Tensegrity Explainer (Post 5)** detail, the body is a tensegrity system that responds well to precise loading rather than constant simulation of 1g.
